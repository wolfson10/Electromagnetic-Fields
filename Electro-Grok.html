1. Vector Calculus Fundamentals

1.1 Coordinate Systems

Cartesian Coordinates (x, y, z)

Position: r = xâₓ + yâᵧ + zâ_z
Volume element: dV = dx dy dz
Surface element: dS = dy dz âₓ or dx dz âᵧ or dx dy â_z

Cylindrical Coordinates (ρ, φ, z)

Position: r = ρâᵨ + zâ_z
Conversions: x = ρcos(φ), y = ρsin(φ), z = z
Volume element: dV = ρ dρ dφ dz
Surface element: dS = ρ dφ dz âᵨ

Spherical Coordinates (r, θ, φ)

Position: r = râᵣ
Conversions: x = r sin(θ)cos(φ), y = r sin(θ)sin(φ), z = r cos(θ)
Volume element: dV = r² sin(θ) dr dθ dφ
Surface element: dS = r² sin(θ) dθ dφ âᵣ

1.2 Vector Operations

Gradient (∇)

∇f = ∂f/∂x âₓ + ∂f/∂y âᵧ + ∂f/∂z â_z

The gradient points in the direction of maximum increase of scalar field f.

Divergence (∇·)

∇·A = ∂Aₓ/∂x + ∂Aᵧ/∂y + ∂A_z/∂z

Measures the "source strength" or "outflow" of a vector field at a point.

Curl (∇×)

∇×A = (∂A_z/∂y - ∂Aᵧ/∂z)âₓ + (∂Aₓ/∂z - ∂A_z/∂x)âᵧ + (∂Aᵧ/∂x - ∂Aₓ/∂y)â_z

Measures the circulation or rotation of a vector field.

Laplacian (∇²)

∇²f = ∂²f/∂x² + ∂²f/∂y² + ∂²f/∂z²

1.3 Integral Theorems

Divergence Theorem (Gauss's Theorem)

∫∫∫V (∇·A) dV = ∮∮S A·dS

Relates volume integral of divergence to surface integral.

Stokes' Theorem

∫∫S (∇×A)·dS = ∮C A·dl

Relates surface integral of curl to line integral.

Gradient Theorem

∫ab (∇f)·dl = f(b) - f(a)
Important Identities:
∇×(∇f) = 0 (curl of gradient is zero)
∇·(∇×A) = 0 (divergence of curl is zero)
∇×(∇×A) = ∇(∇·A) - ∇²A

2. Electrostatics

2.1 Coulomb's Law

F = (1/4πε₀) · (q₁q₂/r²) âᵣ

Where ε₀ = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² F/m (permittivity of free space)

2.2 Electric Field

E = F/q = (1/4πε₀) · (Q/r²) âᵣ

For Point Charge

E = (1/4πε₀) · (q/r²) âᵣ

For Continuous Charge Distribution

E = (1/4πε₀) ∫ (ρ(r')/|r-r'|²) âᵣ dV'

Linear: dE = (λ dl')/(4πε₀r²) âᵣ
Surface: dE = (σ dS')/(4πε₀r²) âᵣ
Volume: dE = (ρ dV')/(4πε₀r²) âᵣ

2.3 Gauss's Law (Integral Form)

∮∮S E·dS = Qenc/ε₀

Differential Form

∇·E = ρ/ε₀

This is one of Maxwell's equations in electrostatics.

2.4 Electric Potential

V(r) = -∫ E·dl

E = -∇V

For Point Charge

V = (1/4πε₀) · (q/r)

For Continuous Distribution

V = (1/4πε₀) ∫ ρ/r dV' = (1/4πε₀) ∫ λ/r dl' = (1/4πε₀) ∫ σ/r dS'

2.5 Poisson's and Laplace's Equations

∇²V = -ρ/ε₀ (Poisson's equation)

∇²V = 0 (Laplace's equation, when ρ = 0)

2.6 Electric Energy

W = (1/2)∫ ρV dV = (ε₀/2)∫ E² dV

Energy Density

uE = (ε₀/2)E² = (1/2)E·D

2.7 Important Field Configurations

Configuration Electric Field Potential
Point charge q E = q/(4πε₀r²) V = q/(4πε₀r)
Infinite line charge (λ) E = λ/(2πε₀ρ) V = -(λ/2πε₀)ln(ρ)
Infinite plane (σ) E = σ/(2ε₀) V = -σz/(2ε₀)
Spherical shell (r > R) E = Q/(4πε₀r²) V = Q/(4πε₀r)
Spherical shell (r < R) E = 0 V = Q/(4πε₀R)

3. Conductors and Dielectrics

3.1 Properties of Conductors

Inside a conductor in equilibrium:
  • E = 0
  • ρ = 0 (no volume charge)
  • V = constant
  • All charge resides on surface
  • E just outside is perpendicular to surface

Surface Charge Density and Field

Eperpendicular = σ/ε₀

E = (σ/ε₀)n̂

3.2 Capacitance

C = Q/V

Common Capacitor Configurations

Type Capacitance
Parallel plate C = ε₀A/d
Cylindrical C = 2πε₀L/ln(b/a)
Spherical C = 4πε₀ab/(b-a)
Isolated sphere C = 4πε₀R

Energy Stored in Capacitor

W = (1/2)CV² = (1/2)QV = Q²/(2C)

3.3 Dielectrics

Polarization

P = dipole moment per unit volume

σb = P·n̂ (bound surface charge)

ρb = -∇·P (bound volume charge)

Electric Displacement Field

D = ε₀E + P

Gauss's Law in Materials

∮ D·dS = Qf,enc

∇·D = ρf

Linear Dielectrics

P = ε₀χeE (electric susceptibility)

D = εE where ε = ε₀(1 + χe) = ε₀εr

εr = relative permittivity (dielectric constant)

Capacitance with Dielectric

C = εrC₀ = εA/d

4. Boundary Conditions

4.1 Electrostatic Boundary Conditions

E⊥,above - E⊥,below = σf/ε₀

D⊥,above - D⊥,below = σf

E∥,above = E∥,below

Vabove = Vbelow
Note: The perpendicular component of E is discontinuous at a surface with free charge, while the parallel component is continuous. The potential V is always continuous.

5. Magnetostatics

5.1 Lorentz Force Law

F = q(E + v×B)

Force on Current Element

dF = I dl×B

F = ∫ I dl×B

5.2 Biot-Savart Law

dB = (μ₀/4π) · (I dl×r̂)/r²

B = (μ₀/4π) ∫ (I dl×r̂)/r²

Where μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A (permeability of free space)

For Current Distributions

B = (μ₀/4π) ∫ (J×r̂)/r² dV'

Surface: B = (μ₀/4π) ∫ (K×r̂)/r² dS'

5.3 Ampère's Law (Integral Form)

∮ B·dl = μ₀Ienc

Differential Form

∇×B = μ₀J

5.4 Magnetic Field Configurations

Configuration Magnetic Field
Infinite straight wire B = μ₀I/(2πρ)
Center of circular loop B = μ₀I/(2R)
Solenoid (interior) B = μ₀nI (n = turns/length)
Toroid B = μ₀NI/(2πr)
Infinite surface current K B = (μ₀/2)K

5.5 Magnetic Vector Potential

B = ∇×A

∇·B = 0 (no magnetic monopoles)

Vector Potential for Current Distributions

A = (μ₀/4π) ∫ J/r dV'

For line current: A = (μ₀I/4π) ∫ dl'/r

5.6 Magnetic Dipole Moment

m = IA (current loop)

τ = m×B (torque)

U = -m·B (potential energy)

Magnetic Dipole Field

B = (μ₀/4π) · [3(m·r̂)r̂ - m]/r³

6. Magnetic Materials

6.1 Magnetization

M = magnetic dipole moment per unit volume

Jb = ∇×M (bound volume current)

Kb = M×n̂ (bound surface current)

6.2 Auxiliary Field H

H = (B/μ₀) - M

Ampère's Law with Magnetization

∮ H·dl = If,enc

∇×H = Jf

6.3 Linear Magnetic Materials

M = χmH (magnetic susceptibility)

B = μH where μ = μ₀(1 + χm) = μ₀μr

μr = relative permeability
Material Types:
• Diamagnetic: χm < 0, μr < 1 (weakly repelled)
• Paramagnetic: χm > 0, μr > 1 (weakly attracted)
• Ferromagnetic: χm >> 0, μr >> 1 (strongly attracted)

6.4 Magnetostatic Boundary Conditions

B⊥,above = B⊥,below

H∥,above - H∥,below = Kf

B∥,above/μabove - B∥,below/μbelow = Kf

6.5 Magnetic Energy

W = (1/2μ₀) ∫ B² dV = (1/2) ∫ B·H dV

Energy Density

uB = B²/(2μ₀) = (1/2)B·H

7. Electromagnetic Induction

7.1 Faraday's Law (Integral Form)

ℰ = -dΦB/dt

ΦB = ∫ B·dS (magnetic flux)

Differential Form

∇×E = -∂B/∂t

This shows that time-varying magnetic fields produce electric fields.

7.2 Motional EMF

ℰ = ∮ (v×B)·dl

7.3 Inductance

Self-Inductance

ΦB = LI

ℰ = -L(dI/dt)

L = ΦB/I

Mutual Inductance

M₁₂ = M₂₁ = M = Φ₁₂/I₂ = Φ₂₁/I₁

ℰ₁ = -M(dI₂/dt)

Common Inductance Formulas

Configuration Inductance
Solenoid L = μ₀n²V = μ₀n²Al
Toroid L = μ₀N²h/(2π)ln(b/a)
Coaxial cable L = (μ₀l/2π)ln(b/a)

7.4 Magnetic Energy in Inductors

W = (1/2)LI²

7.5 Displacement Current

Jd = ε₀(∂E/∂t) = ∂D/∂t

Ampère-Maxwell Law

∮ B·dl = μ₀(Ienc + ε₀dΦE/dt)

∇×B = μ₀J + μ₀ε₀(∂E/∂t)

8. Maxwell's Equations

8.1 Maxwell's Equations in Vacuum

Integral Form

1. ∮ E·dS = Q/ε₀

2. ∮ B·dS = 0

3. ∮ E·dl = -dΦB/dt

4. ∮ B·dl = μ₀I + μ₀ε₀dΦE/dt

Differential Form

1. ∇·E = ρ/ε₀

2. ∇·B = 0

3. ∇×E = -∂B/∂t

4. ∇×B = μ₀J + μ₀ε₀∂E/∂t

8.2 Maxwell's Equations in Matter

1. ∇·D = ρf

2. ∇·B = 0

3. ∇×E = -∂B/∂t

4. ∇×H = Jf + ∂D/∂t

8.3 Continuity Equation

∇·J + ∂ρ/∂t = 0

∮ J·dS = -dQ/dt

8.4 Boundary Conditions (General)

Electric Fields:
• E⊥,2 - E⊥,1 = σf/ε₀
• D⊥,2 - D⊥,1 = σf
• E∥,2 = E∥,1

Magnetic Fields:
• B⊥,2 = B⊥,1
• H∥,2 - H∥,1 = Kf
• (1/μ₂)B∥,2 - (1/μ₁)B∥,1 = Kf

9. Electromagnetic Waves

9.1 Wave Equation

∇²E = μ₀ε₀(∂²E/∂t²)

∇²B = μ₀ε₀(∂²B/∂t²)

In General Form

∇²E = με(∂²E/∂t²) + μσ(∂E/∂t)

9.2 Plane Wave Solutions

E = E₀ cos(k·r - ωt + φ)

B = B₀ cos(k·r - ωt + φ)

Complex Notation

Ẽ = Ẽ₀ ei(k·r - ωt)

B̃ = B̃₀ ei(k·r - ωt)

9.3 Wave Properties

v = ω/k = 1/√(με) (phase velocity)

c = 1/√(μ₀ε₀) = 3 × 10⁸ m/s (speed of light in vacuum)

v = c/n where n = √(μrεr) (refractive index)

λ = 2π/k = vT = v/f (wavelength)

9.4 Wave Relations

E ⊥ B ⊥ k (transverse waves)

B = E/v = √(με)E

B = (k×E)/ω = (1/v)(k̂×E)

9.5 Polarization

Linear Polarization

E = E₀ cos(kz - ωt) x̂

Circular Polarization

E = E₀[cos(kz - ωt) x̂ ± sin(kz - ωt) ŷ]

Elliptical Polarization

E = Ex cos(kz - ωt) x̂ + Ey cos(kz - ωt + δ) ŷ

9.6 Reflection and Transmission

Normal Incidence

Reflection coefficient: R = [(n₁ - n₂)/(n₁ + n₂)]²

Transmission coefficient: T = 4n₁n₂/(n₁ + n₂)²

R + T = 1

Fresnel Equations (oblique incidence)

r⊥ = (n₁cosθi - n₂cosθt)/(n₁cosθi + n₂cosθt)

r∥ = (n₂cosθi - n₁cosθt)/(n₂cosθi + n₁cosθt)

t⊥ = 2n₁cosθi/(n₁cosθi + n₂cosθt)

t∥ = 2n₁cosθi/(n₂cosθi + n₁cosθt)

Brewster's Angle

tan(θB) = n₂/n₁

At θB: r∥ = 0 (no reflection for parallel polarization)

Total Internal Reflection

Critical angle: sin(θc) = n₂/n₁ (when n₁ > n₂)

For θi > θc: complete reflection

10. Energy and Momentum in EM Waves

10.1 Poynting Vector

S = (1/μ₀)E×B = E×H

Represents electromagnetic energy flux (W/m²)

For Plane Waves

|S| = (1/μ₀v)E² = (ε/μ)½E² = cε₀E² (in vacuum)

10.2 Energy Density

u = uE + uB = (ε₀/2)E² + (1/2μ₀)B²

For plane waves: uE = uB, so u = ε₀E² = B²/μ₀

10.3 Intensity

I = <|S|> = (1/2)cε₀E₀² = (1/2μ₀c)B₀² (time average)

10.4 Poynting's Theorem

-∂u/∂t = ∇·S + J·E

Integral Form

-dW/dt = ∮ S·dA + ∫ J·E dV

Energy conservation: rate of decrease of EM energy = energy flow out + ohmic losses

10.5 Radiation Pressure

Momentum density: g = S/c² = ε₀E×B

Radiation pressure (complete absorption): P = I/c = S/c

Radiation pressure (perfect reflection): P = 2I/c = 2S/c

10.6 Maxwell Stress Tensor

Tij = ε₀(EiEj - ½δijE²) + (1/μ₀)(BiBj - ½δijB²)

Force per unit volume

f = ρE + J×B = ∇·T - ε₀μ₀∂S/∂t

11. Additional Important Topics

11.1 Electromagnetic Potentials

E = -∇V - ∂A/∂t

B = ∇×A

Gauge Transformations

A' = A + ∇λ

V' = V - ∂λ/∂t

Lorenz Gauge

∇·A + με∂V/∂t = 0

Coulomb Gauge

∇·A = 0

11.2 Retarded Potentials

V(r,t) = (1/4πε₀) ∫ ρ(r',tret)/|r-r'| dV'

A(r,t) = (μ₀/4π) ∫ J(r',tret)/|r-r'| dV'

where tret = t - |r-r'|/c

11.3 Dipole Radiation

Electric Dipole

E = (μ₀/4πr)[n̂×(n̂×p̈)]

B = (μ₀/4πrc)[n̂×p̈]

Power radiated: P = (μ₀p̈²)/(6πc) = (μ₀ω⁴p₀²)/(12πc)

Larmor Formula

P = (μ₀q²a²)/(6πc) = (q²a²)/(6πε₀c³)

Power radiated by accelerating point charge

11.4 Skin Depth

d = 1/√(πfμσ) = √(2/ωμσ)

Depth at which field amplitude decreases to 1/e in a conductor

Summary of Key Concepts

Fundamental Laws

  • Coulomb's Law: Foundation of electrostatics
  • Gauss's Law: Relates electric field to charge distribution
  • Ampère's Law: Relates magnetic field to current
  • Faraday's Law: Time-varying B produces E
  • Maxwell's Addition: Time-varying E produces B (displacement current)

Problem-Solving Strategies

  • Use Gauss's Law for symmetric charge distributions
  • Use Ampère's Law for symmetric current distributions
  • Apply boundary conditions at interfaces
  • Use potential methods when dealing with conservative fields
  • Apply superposition for complex configurations
  • Check units and dimensions throughout calculations
